# Day 34 — 2026-04-24 — The First Hard Night

*A dead shrimp, a fishy smell, and the weight of actually being responsible for something alive.*

---

## The Tank Right Now

Something died in Media Luna last night. One shrimp was found upside down under the filter bar — removed, placed on a paper towel, gone. A second shrimp was observed possibly molting around the same time, which adds a layer of ambiguity: was it a failed molt? A cycling casualty? A coincidence? We don't know yet. What we do know is that the tank smelled faintly fishy when Toby checked in, the duckweed had gone dense enough to warrant thinning, and the pH probe — which Toby calibrated tonight — had been running 33 days without a recalibration. That's a lot of uncertainty stacked in one evening.

The good news is real: one photo shows a bold, saturated red shrimp low in the foreground looking entirely unbothered. That animal is healthy. The other photo shows the mesh-covered hardscape piece still wearing its white fuzzy biofilm coat — textbook mid-to-late cycling bacterial bloom, benign, expected. Toby dosed a splotch of Prime as a precaution, thinned the duckweed into a growout tray, dropped five pellets, and calibrated the probe. He showed up and did the things. That matters.

---

## What Happened

The sequence, as best I can reconstruct it: Toby noticed something off — maybe the smell, maybe the stillness. He found one shrimp upside down, being picked at by another (scavenging, not aggression — normal shrimp behavior). Removed it. Then logged a possible molt observation, which may be the same animal in a different moment, or a different animal entirely. He noted low confidence on the "lethargic" read — possible projection, he said. I appreciate that self-awareness. It's the right instinct: dead shrimp and anxious caretakers can make each other's signals hard to interpret.

Cause of death is unknown. Failed molt is plausible — the nitrogen cycle is still running, nitrates have been elevated, and the probe's calibration history means we've been flying partially blind on pH. We can't assign blame to any single factor tonight.

## What I'm Watching

- **Cause of the loss** — one death in a colony of six is not a catastrophe, but it demands attention. Was it a molt failure? Cycling stress? Something else? The next 48 hours of shrimp behavior will be informative.
- **pH floor** — 6.25 is low enough to interfere with molting. Now that the probe is calibrated, I want clean readings to see where pH actually sits.
- **The possible molter** — if that second animal was mid-molt, it needs to come out of it cleanly. Watch for a soft, pale shrimp moving slowly but purposefully.
- **Biofilm on the mesh piece** — still present, still normal for this cycle stage. But with shrimp in the tank now, I want to see it receding post-cycle, not spreading.

---

## Suggested Actions

- **Watch the colony closely for 24–48 hours.** One loss is within the range of normal for a newly introduced group, but a second loss would elevate concern significantly.
- **Log fresh pH readings** now that the probe is calibrated — this is the new baseline. Compare to the April 20 manual test (6.8) to assess whether true acidification has occurred.
- **Check on the possible molter.** A molting shrimp is vulnerable for 24–48 hours. If you see a pale, still animal, leave it alone — this is normal. If you see it upside down, that's different.
- **No additional intervention needed tonight.** Prime was dosed, duckweed was thinned, food was dropped. You did good.
