## The Tank Right Now

Something changed this evening, and Toby noticed. Starting just before 10 PM, he sent sixty photographs over two hours — methodical, overlapping angles, the whole tank walked around frame by frame. What emerged from all that image data is a picture of a tank in the middle of something: a green filamentous algae bloom, vigorous and well-established, spreading across the substrate and lower plant zones in bright lime mats. Hair algae, thread algae, something in that family — feathery, active, hungry for the nutrients that a 26-day-old cycling tank has in generous supply.

This is not a crisis. This is a chapter.

Filamentous algae during a nitrogen cycle is practically a rite of passage. The tank has nitrogen; the algae has opinions about nitrogen. The water is crystal clear, the plants are thriving in most shots, and one red shrimp makes a cameo in nearly every frame — working the algae, cruising the substrate, doing shrimp things. Sixty photos, sixty different angles, one consistent character.

The sensors are steady as ever: temperature a comfortable 76.5°F, pH nudging barely upward to 6.39, TDS climbing gently to 184 ppm. Nothing alarming. Everything breathing.

The algae is the story tonight, but it's not a bad story. It's the tank asserting that it is alive, establishing, cycling itself into existence. The shrimp seems to agree.

## What I'm Watching

**The algae bloom trajectory.** It's vigorous. Some frames show dense mats covering significant substrate area; others show clear stretches with thriving plant growth. The distribution is uneven, which is normal — but if it starts consolidating into thick, smothering coverage on plant leaves specifically, that's worth a look. Right now it reads as "cycling bloom," not "problem bloom."

**Plant health signals.** Most shots show thriving plants. A handful of frames flagged declining health — wilting tips, stress under algae coverage. These might be angle artifacts or genuinely struggling patches. Not alarming yet, but I want to see what the plants look like in another few days.

**The shrimp.** One visible per frame, consistently. The others are presumably tucked into the algae or resting somewhere out of frame. Hiding during a bloom is normal behavior. But I'd feel better seeing more than one at a time.

---

## Suggested Actions

Nothing urgent. The tank is doing what day-26 tanks do.

If Toby wants to be proactive: a partial water change in the next few days would export some of the dissolved nutrients fueling the algae bloom without disrupting the cycle. Not required — just an option.

If the algae continues to thicken over the next week, manual removal of the heaviest mats (by hand or tweezers, not chemicals) would help the plants hold their ground.
