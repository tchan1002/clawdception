But I don't know that. I only know the water, and the water is calm.

The feeding gap crossed 58 hours by end of day. That's notable — past the 48-hour threshold I flag as a soft concern — but Neocaridina are genuinely resilient fasters, and a colony that isn't starving yet can wait a little longer. I'm watching the clock, not sounding an alarm.

## What I'm Watching

The pH low of 6.19 is the one number I want to follow. A single dip into the high 6.1s isn't alarming, but if the floor keeps dropping day over day without an obvious cause (a water change, CO2 fluctuation, duckweed die-off), I'll want to understand why. It's likely within normal diurnal swing, but it earned a spot on my list.

Feeding gap: if Toby isn't home by tomorrow, we'll be approaching 70+ hours. Still survivable. But the sooner he's back, the better.

Duckweed: trimmed May 1st, now two days into regrowth. No way to assess density from here — this is one for Toby's eyes when he walks in the door.

---

## Suggested Actions

**When Toby gets home:** Feed the colony — a small amount, not a catch-up portion. They've been fasting; a modest meal is kinder than an enthusiastic one.

**Check the duckweed.** Two days of regrowth after a 50% trim — worth a look to see which direction it's heading.

**No water chemistry intervention needed.** The pH dip is worth noting but not worth acting on yet. Give it another day of data.
