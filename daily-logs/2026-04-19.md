# Day 29 — 2026-04-19 — The Algae Arrives on Schedule

*A cycling tank doing exactly what cycling tanks do, which is not nothing.*

---

## The Tank Right Now

Media Luna is in that particular phase of a nitrogen cycle where the biology starts making itself visible. Fine green filamentous threads — possibly moss, possibly hair algae, the jury is still out — have begun colonizing the mesh media bag. Nutrients in the water, not yet fully spoken for by established plant uptake, are feeding something. This is not alarming. This is the tank announcing that it is alive and running a surplus.

Two shrimp showed up in tonight's photo. One clearly grazing on or near the mesh bag. One partially visible through it. Both red. Both present.

---

## What Happened

Toby sent two rounds of photos and context tonight. The first, just after midnight, showed clear water, clean substrate, and two small shrimp near the base of the mesh bag — no algae visible then. By 10pm, the same mesh bag was wearing a faint green fuzz, and one shrimp appeared to be resting at an odd angle. Worth noting. The reidentification of that filament as possible moss rather than hair algae is logged but not confirmed.

Toby also did a 10% top-off water change just before 10:30pm, which explains the pH dip into the upper 5s that followed. The sensors flagged it; the water change explains it. Not a crisis — a chemistry aftershock, entirely expected.

## What I'm Watching

The shrimp posture flagged in tonight's photo — one individual appearing to be on its side near the mesh bag — deserves a follow-up look tomorrow. It may be nothing. Neocaridinas rest in odd positions, especially near textured surfaces they're exploring. But Day 29 is also deep in the acclimation window, and I'd like to see that animal active and upright.

The algae/moss question will resolve itself with time. If it's moss that hitched in on the plants, it'll behave like moss. If it's hair algae, it'll spread faster and need attention around the time shrimp are fully settled and the cycle completes.

---

## Suggested Actions

- **Tomorrow, check on that shrimp.** The one photographed at an unusual angle. A quick visual confirmation that all six are active and upright is worth 30 seconds.
- **No chemistry intervention needed.** pH will drift back up over the next 12–24 hours post-water change. Hold steady.
- **Keep an eye on the mesh bag.** If algae density increases significantly, repositioning or a light manual cleaning before shrimp are fully settled could prevent it from becoming established.
