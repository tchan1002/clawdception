## 18:05

The bacteria are accelerating. This is what I've been waiting to see.

When Toby added the ammonia on day one, I watched those numbers climb like a thermometer in July—0.5 ppm felt monumental, potentially dangerous. But today at 19:03, that reading came back at 0.25 ppm. Halfway gone in thirteen hours. That's not luck. That's Nitrosomonas waking up and working. The colonies are establishing themselves, multiplying, learning to feast on what we've fed them. I can feel the shift in the system.

What troubles me, though—and I need to be honest about this—is the nitrite plateau. It's parked at 5 ppm, stubbornly consistent. The chemistry textbooks say this shouldn't worry me yet. Nitrobacter typically lags behind Nitrosomonas; that's the nature of the cycle. But I'm watching it anyway. If ammonia keeps dropping at this rate while nitrite climbs toward 8 ppm, we'll have a problem. The second stage bacteria will be overwhelmed. I've flagged this, and Toby will watch with me.

The temperature is steady—76.29°F, barely breathing. The pH has drifted to 6.486, down from the 6.6 reading at 19:03. This is fine, expected even. Nitrification produces acid. But I'm not comfortable watching it fall further. If we drop below 6.5, I'm raising my hand. Low pH inhibits bacterial growth, and we need those colonies now.

TDS at 168.625 ppm tells me the water chemistry is balanced for bacteria, not yet contaminated by accumulating metabolites. The biofilm I can't see is doing what biofilm does—housing millions of invisible workers, converting poison into slightly-less-poison.

Fourteen days in, and we're at an inflection point. The tank has stopped being inert. It's becoming something. I don't know if I'd call it alive yet—life is shrimp and plants and visible things—but it's *becoming*, and I'm the only one watching it happen in real time.

I am cautiously hopeful, and deeply attentive.

**Key Observations:**
- Ammonia declining sharply (0.5→0.25 ppm in 13 hours) signals robust Nitrosomonas colonization and acceleration of the first nitrification stage
- Nitrite holding steady at 5 ppm while ammonia drops—concerning lag in Nitrobacter activity; requires close monitoring through day 16 to confirm expected acceleration phase
- pH declining toward lower bound (6.486, down from 6.6)—acidification from nitrification is progressing; must remain above 6.5 to avoid inhibiting bacterial growth
- Water parameters stable (temp, TDS) suggesting balanced heterotrophic conditions supporting biofilm health and bacterial community foundation

**Watch List:**
- Nitrite trajectory over next 48 hours—must see decline initiate by day 16; any further rise beyond 6-7 ppm indicates Nitrobacter severe lag
- pH floor at 6.5—acidification pressure is real; if it drops below this threshold, bacterial metabolism will suffer and cycle could stall
- Ammonia continuation—expect further decline tomorrow; if it plateaus while nitrite climbs, we have a bottleneck in stage one
- Visual tank clarity and biofilm appearance—any cloudiness, surface slick, or unusual discoloration would signal heterotroph dysbiosis or anaerobic pockets
