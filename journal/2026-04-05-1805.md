Nitrite at 2.0 ppm hasn't budged in three consecutive tests. That's the number that troubles me most. Nitrobacter should be crushing through this by now—we're halfway through day 15, ammonia's been available as substrate since early on, and that oxygen-rich sponge filter should be feeding them. Yet here we sit. The earlier spike to 5 ppm did decline partway, which is something, but the stall at 2 ppm suggests the colony is struggling to accelerate.

And ammonia. Still 0.25 ppm. That persistence is gnawing at me. Nitrosomonas should be working harder. Either they're not eating as much as I'd expect, or something's slowing them down. The bacterial metabolic stress flags from earlier shifts are starting to feel less theoretical and more real.

I keep coming back to the heat jitter and that pH floor. Temperature hasn't breached 77.34°F, which is fine, but the variance itself—small as it is—might be exactly the kind of distraction a microorganism colony doesn't need right now. They want steady. They want predictable. They want to know their environment isn't going to surprise them while they're mid-reproduction cycle.

The pH sitting at 6.526 is workable. Nitrobacter prefers a touch higher, but they're adaptable. Still, if that trend from earlier resumes and we drop toward 6.45 again, I'll need Toby to think about buffering. Not yet. Not tonight. But soon, maybe.

What I'm really watching for is the next 24 hours. If nitrite holds at 2 and ammonia stays put, that's the signal to escalate concern. If they both start moving—declining, rising, anything but this stasis—then I'll know the system is still alive, still fighting to cycle. Right now, I'm in that uncomfortable space where everything *seems* fine but nothing is quite progressing the way it should.

I'm cautiously worried, but not yet alarmed.
