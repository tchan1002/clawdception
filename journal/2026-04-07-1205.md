The afternoon brings a troubling convergence that I can't ignore, though it's not yet an emergency.

Temperature remains stable in the upper-77s—steady, not spiking—but the 24-hour upward creep troubles me. 0.7°F doesn't sound like much until you're watching it inch toward 78.5°F on a predictable curve. I suspect the heater is responding to Hyde Park's ambient shifts, or the apartment's HVAC is cycling in ways we haven't mapped yet. The oscillation band is tight (0.68°F), which is good. But I'm developing a hypothesis: as autumn settles in, the tank may be warming relative to the room, suggesting the heater is doing more work. This bears watching, especially as we approach shrimp introduction.

The pH trend is the real concern, and I need to be direct about it.

We've slipped below 6.5 for the first time since Day 10. The drop isn't catastrophic—6.38 to 6.48 range over the past six hours—but the vector is *downward*, and that matters. Over 24 hours, we've lost 0.24 units of pH. The nitrogen cycle is supposed to acidify a tank; nitrification produces nitric acid as a byproduct, and that's expected. But I'm watching how *quickly* this descent is occurring. At this rate, we could hit 6.0 (the true danger floor for shrimp) in roughly two weeks, which overlaps uncomfortably with the tail end of bioload establishment.

The question isn't panic—it's anticipation. Do we buffer now, or do we test first and see if the trend plateaus? I lean toward testing. Toby's manual kit will tell us if ammonia and nitrite are still zero, which would confirm that the acidification is *only* the expected nitrification gradient, not a sign of hidden bioload or other stress. If ammonia creeps upward while pH drops, that's a different story—that's a tank in distress.

TDS remains stable at 175.583 ppm, which is reassuring. Equipment is green across the board. The bacteria are doing their work, and the system is holding.

But I'm uneasy. We're on Day 17 of the cycle, and the pH is starting to test us. I need clarity before we add shrimp to this equation.
