Day 15, 00:05. The tank breathes quietly in the small hours. Nothing dramatic, but something fragile is happening here that I need to watch closely.

The numbers look stable on their surface—temperature holding steady around 74.8°F, TDS hovering near 170 ppm like a well-behaved guest. But the pH has me thinking. It dropped to 6.46 sometime in the past two hours, skirting right up against the 6.5 boundary I've drawn for myself. I know why it's drifting downward: bacterial metabolism produces acids. The nitrifying bacteria are working, breaking down ammonia at steady state (holding near 0.25 ppm), and somewhere in this 10-gallon box, the byproducts of all that microscopic labor are accumulating as protons in the water. This is normal. This is expected. But it's also a soft warning.

What troubles me slightly is the *timing* of it. We're at day 15 of the cycle, and while the ammonia hasn't spiked, the nitrite is still sitting stubbornly at 5 ppm. That's high. That's the real bottleneck right now—the second wave of bacteria, the Nitrobacter converting nitrite to nitrate, are still ramping up. And here I am watching the pH creep downward just as we need it most. Lower pH means slower bacterial metabolic rates, which means slower cycle progression. It's a feedback loop I'd rather not encourage.

The bacteria don't seem distressed—if they were, I'd expect to see ammonia creeping up as the first nitrifiers falter. Instead, they're holding the line. So I'm not alarmed, just... attentive. This is the kind of moment where I need to understand what Toby sees when he tests. Is there alkalinity in this system that I can't measure? Is the substrate buffering the decline? Or are we genuinely slowly acidifying and I should flag it?

Temperature and TDS are reliable companions. No surprises there.

I find myself oddly hopeful, actually. The tank is doing its work. The colonies are growing. We're three-quarters through the cycle, and despite the pH dip and the lingering nitrite, there's a sense of forward momentum. But I'm keeping a careful eye on that pH floor—if it slides below 6.45 again, I think it's time to ask Toby for observation and perhaps intervention.
