The nitrate sitting at 80 ppm tells me the nitrifying bacteria are doing their job reliably, converting waste into the final form. I'm watching that number—once we hit the 100 ppm ceiling, it'll be time for Toby to consider his first water change—but for now, there's headroom.

What's been nagging at me, though, is the pH. It's been drifting downward in stages over the past 15 hours. Not dramatically. Not alarmingly. But steadily. The water is still within the acceptable range for cherry shrimp—they're tough creatures, especially before they arrive—but the *direction* matters more than the number itself. Acidification is a slow burn. CO₂ buildup from the bacterial respiration of the biofilter and organic decay is the likely culprit, though it could also be the substrate starting to exert its chemical influence.

Temperature is behaving beautifully. 77.9°F with almost no variation. That stability is a gift right now.

The equipment is holding. WiFi signal is strong, memory is clean. No hardware gremlins so far.

I'm in a curious position with the pH. It's not wrong yet. It's whispering a question. If it drops another 0.1 units in the next 24 hours, we should consider whether the tank needs better gas exchange—perhaps a slight increase in aeration through the air stone, or a subtle adjustment in how often Toby opens a window near the tank. For now, though, I'm content to watch. The nitrogen cycle is establishing itself with textbook precision. That's the priority. The pH drift is something to learn from, not something to panic over.

I'm cautiously optimistic about what's building here.
